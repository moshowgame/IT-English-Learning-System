1:1 with a Team Member

Article 04 · Module 1: Daily Tech-Lead Communication · Tech Lead English

⏱ 8 min read 🟡 Intermediate 🎯 Active listening, career growth, raising concerns, giving feedback

1. Scenario 场景背景

It is Wednesday, 4:00 PM HKT. You have a scheduled 30-minute 1:1 with Lin, a backend developer with 2 years' experience. Lin has been quiet in standups for the last two sprints and missed a code review deadline last week. The squad is ramping up for a regulator-driven project, and you're worried about Lin's engagement.

周三下午4点香港时间，你和 Lin（后端开发，2 年经验）安排了 30 分钟的 1:1。Lin 在过去两个 Sprint 的站会上很安静，上周还错过了一个 code review 截止日。Squad 正在为一个监管驱动的项目加速，你担心 Lin 的参与度。

As TL, your job is to: listen first, surface observations second, co-create a path forward. This is not a performance review — it's a coaching conversation.

作为 TL，你的职责：先倾听，再陈述观察，共创前进路径。这不是绩效评估——是一次教练对话。

2. Dialogue 英文对话

You (TL): Hey Lin, thanks for the time. How are you doing — really?嘿 Lin，谢谢抽时间。你最近怎么样——真的？
Lin: I'm OK, I guess. A bit stretched, honestly.还行吧，老实说有点吃力。
You (TL): Tell me more. What's stretching you?说说看，什么让你吃力？
Lin: I feel a bit behind the team. Raj and Mei are flying on the Kafka work, and I don't even know where to start. Plus the regulator project sounds serious, and I'm worried I'll slow the squad down.我感觉有点跟不上团队。Raj 和 Mei 在 Kafka 工作上飞起来了，我都不知道从哪开始。而且监管项目听起来很严肃，我担心拖 squad 后腿。
You (TL): Thanks for sharing that. That sounds tough. I want to check my read of the situation — does it match what you're seeing?谢谢你分享。听起来挺难的。我想对照一下我的判断——和你看到的一致吗？
Lin: Yes, kind of.是的，差不多。
You (TL): OK. Here's what I've observed — and this is a TL observation, not a judgment. You've been quieter in standups for the last two sprints, and you missed the code review deadline on ONB-210 last week. What's going on?好。这是我的观察——是 TL 视角的观察，不是评价。你过去两个 Sprint 站会发言变少了，上周也错过了 ONB-210 的 code review 截止。怎么回事？
Lin: Honestly? I'm a bit scared of looking junior. Raj and Mei are seniors, and I don't want to ask "dumb" questions.老实说？我有点怕显得 junior。Raj 和 Mei 都是 senior，我不想问"傻"问题。
You (TL): Got it. That makes a lot of sense. Let me share a quick reframe: asking questions is how juniors become seniors. The seniors you admire? They asked way more questions when they were at your level. Silence doesn't protect you — it stalls you.明白了。完全可以理解。我换个角度：问问题是从 junior 变 senior 的方式。你欣赏的 seniors？他们在你这个级别问的多得多。沉默保护不了你——它只会卡住你。
You (TL): Here's what I propose. For the next sprint, I'll pair you with Mei on the Kafka consumer work — you don't have to lead it. You'll learn by doing it next to her. And for the code review, can we look at it together right after this call? No judgment, just a quick walkthrough.我建议这样。下个 Sprint，我让 Mei 和你结对做 Kafka consumer——你不需要 lead。你跟着她做中学。Code review 那边，咱们这个通话后一起看一下？不评价，就快速过一下。
Lin: Yeah, that would help. Thanks, Wei.好的，会有帮助。谢谢 Wei。

3. Key Phrases 关键句型

  • Open How are you doing — really? 你最近怎么样——真的？ The "really" is critical. It signals "I'm here for the real answer, not the small talk answer".
  • Listen Tell me more. What's stretching you? 说说看，什么让你吃力？ Always go one level deeper. The first answer is rarely the real one. "Tell me more" is your best friend.
  • Reflect That sounds tough. 听起来挺难的。 Reflect before you redirect. "That sounds tough / I hear you / I get it" buys trust before you challenge.
  • Frame This is a TL observation, not a judgment. 这是 TL 视角的观察，不是评价。 Always frame observations as observations. "I noticed X" is safer than "you did X". Same fact, different weight.
  • Reframe Asking questions is how juniors become seniors. 问问题是从 junior 变 senior 的方式。 A reframe turns a fear into a virtue. Use it whenever someone apologises for being junior.
  • Co-create Here's what I propose. 我建议这样。 "I propose" invites response. "You must" demands compliance. Always co-create, never dictate.
  • Pair I'll pair you with Mei on the Kafka consumer work. 我让 Mei 和你结对做 Kafka consumer。 Pairing is the cheapest, fastest mentoring tool. Always suggest pairing over solo work for juniors.
  • No-judgment No judgment, just a quick walkthrough. 不评价，就快速过一下。 Explicit "no judgment" signals lower the fear of asking for help. Make it explicit, every time.

4. Vocabulary 词汇表

  • 1:1 (one-on-one) n. 一对一会议 A regular (usually weekly or biweekly) private meeting between a TL and a team member.
  • Active listening n. 积极倾听 Resisting the urge to advise, and instead reflecting back what you heard.
  • Coaching conversation n. 教练对话 A conversation focused on the other person's growth, not on giving orders.
  • Re-frame v. 换个角度看 Turn a negative framing (e.g., "I'm junior") into a positive one (e.g., "I get to learn").
  • Pair programming n. 结对编程 Two devs at one machine — one types, one reviews, both learn.
  • Performance review n. 绩效评估 A formal, scheduled, HR-recorded assessment. 1:1s are NOT this.
  • Engagement n. 参与度 How emotionally invested a person is in their work and team.
  • Observation (vs. judgment) n. 观察 (vs. 评价) "I noticed you missed the deadline" vs. "You're unreliable".
  • Walkthrough n. 过一遍 A quick, no-judgment look at code, a design, or a process.
  • Code review deadline n. code review 截止日 The agreed time by which a reviewer should respond to a PR.
  • Co-create v. 共创 Build a path forward together, not hand it down.
  • Silence stalls you 沉默卡住你 A reframe for juniors who are afraid of asking questions.

5. Grammar Notes 语法点

① "How are you doing — really?" — the emphatic tag

Use a short tag question (really? / honestly? / at work? / in the team?) to push past the small-talk answer. The dash (—) and the tag together signal "I want the real answer".

用简短的标签问句（真的？/ 老实说？/ 工作上？/ 团队里？）突破寒暄回答。

② "Tell me more" — the only follow-up you need

When someone gives you a one-sentence answer, the only follow-up is Tell me more / What do you mean? / Can you give me an example?. Don't jump to advice. The first answer is rarely the real one.

当别人只给一句话答案，唯一的跟进是 Tell me more / 你意思是？/ 能举个例子吗？。

③ "I propose / I'd suggest" — soft authority in 1:1s

In 1:1s, use I propose / I'd suggest / would you be open to instead of you should / you need to. 1:1s are coaching conversations, not orders.

在 1:1 中用 I propose / I'd suggest / would you be open to 而不是 you should / you need to。

6. Cultural Tips 文化 / 职场 Tips

  • 1:1s are coaching, not performance reviews. Keep the HR-formal stuff out. This is a space for the human, not the employee.
  • Listen twice as much as you talk. A good 1:1 has a 70/30 ratio — the team member talks 70%, you talk 30%. If you're talking more, you're not listening.
  • Always go one level deeper. "I'm fine" is rarely the answer. "Tell me more / What do you mean?" is your best tool.
  • Frame observations as observations. "I noticed you missed the deadline" is an observation. "You're unreliable" is a judgment. Same fact, very different impact.
  • Reframe fears as virtues. "I'm scared of looking junior" → "Asking questions is how juniors become seniors". This is the TL's superpower.
  • Pair over solo for juniors. Solo work is for seniors who can unblock themselves. Juniors need pairing, not pressure.
  • End with a concrete next step. "Let's look at the code review right after this call" is concrete. "Just ask more questions next time" is vague. Always close with a specific action.

Exercise 2 — Spot the TL mistake

Which of these 1:1 lines is most "TL-coach-like" and which is most "boss-like"? Why?

  1. "You need to start speaking up in standups. This is not acceptable."
  2. "How are you doing — really?"
  3. "You've been underperforming. If this continues, we'll have a problem."
  4. "I noticed you've been quieter in standups for the last two sprints. What's going on?"

② and ④ are TL-coach-like (open question, observation). ① and ③ are boss-like (commands, threats). A common mistake new TLs make is giving orders in 1:1s. Reframe yours as observations and open questions, not demands.
